## Why most prompts fail

&ldquo;What are you grateful for?&rdquo; and &ldquo;Describe your perfect day&rdquo; aren&apos;t bad questions. They&apos;re just too comfortable. Good prompts should make you pause — not because they&apos;re dramatic, but because they require honest thought.

The best prompts sit at the edge of what you already know about yourself. They surface assumptions, contradictions, and things you&apos;ve been avoiding.

## Prompts for clarity

When your thoughts feel tangled, these help untangle them. &ldquo;What am I avoiding right now, and why?&rdquo; cuts through procrastination. &ldquo;If I already knew the answer, what would it be?&rdquo; bypasses overthinking.

&ldquo;What would I do if I wasn&apos;t afraid?&rdquo; is a clich&eacute; for a reason — it works. The fear you name on the page loses some of its power.

## Prompts for self-knowledge

&ldquo;What drained my energy this week?&rdquo; and &ldquo;What gave me energy?&rdquo; are deceptively simple. Track the answers over a month and you&apos;ll learn more about yourself than most personality tests reveal.

&ldquo;When did I last change my mind about something important?&rdquo; is uncomfortable. That discomfort is the point.

## Prompts for difficult seasons

During hard times, gentle prompts matter. &ldquo;What do I need right now that I&apos;m not giving myself?&rdquo; creates space for self-compassion without requiring a specific emotion.

&ldquo;What is one small thing I can control today?&rdquo; works when everything feels chaotic. Not as a fix, but as a foothold.

## Using prompts well

Don&apos;t answer a prompt every day. Use them when you&apos;re stuck — when you sit down to write and nothing comes. The rest of the time, freewriting is usually better.

Think of prompts as a spare key. You don&apos;t need it every day, but when you&apos;re locked out, it gets you back in.
